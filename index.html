Key Statistics

Maine PIT Count · Jan 2025
Understanding Point-in-Time (PIT) counts: The annual PIT count is a federally mandated snapshot of homelessness on a single January night. It captures people in shelters, transitional housing, and outdoor locations. It does not capture people in informal arrangements (doubled-up, couch-surfing). Pandemic-era motel programs inflated 2022–2023 counts; their expiration deflates 2024–2025 figures. Interpret trends with caution.

Racial disparities

Black, African American, and African individuals are significantly overrepresented in Maine's unhoused population relative to their share of the statewide general population. So are American Indian or Alaska Native and Multi-Racial residents. The 2025 PIT count recorded a significant decrease in this group (from 1,141 to 757) partially attributed to successful employment and housing transitions for asylum-seeking individuals. These disparities reflect broader structural inequities in housing access.
Note on asylum seekers: Portland has seen a significant influx of asylum-seeking families in recent years. In 2022, the state, City of Portland, and Catholic Charities Maine launched a transitional housing program in Saco, which served 192 households (737 people); 81% of those who left transitioned to permanent housing.

The Oxford Street Shelter has relocated to the Riverton/Riverside neighborhood, opening a new 179-bed Homeless Services Center in 2023. Most shelters across Maine report being at or near full capacity on most nights, according to Preble Street's senior leadership.

Warming Centers (Seasonal)

During winter months, supplemental warming centers open across Portland and Cumberland County. In fall 2024, MaineHousing distributed over $2 million in grants (approved by Gov. Mills and the Legislature) for the operation of warming centers statewide. Warming center locations and hours vary — contact 2-1-1 for current information.

Key findings from MaineHousing 2025 report

  • Total 2025 PIT count: 2,413 — down 282 (10%) from 2024
  • Families with children: 14.9% of all households; <0.4% unsheltered
  • Veteran count increased ~10% between 2022–2025
  • Catholic Charities/Saco transitional program: 81% of leavers housed permanently
  • 775 affordable units built in 2024; 727 more projected for 2025
  • Average stay length rose 38% in 2024 — a "concerning shift"
  • Shelters across the state filling quickly as capacity increases

What the data does NOT capture

"Hidden homelessness" — people doubled-up in overcrowded housing, couch-surfing, or in informal arrangements — is not counted in PIT totals. HUD does not classify most of these situations as homelessness. MaineHousing cautions that the sharp numerical drop from 2022 to 2025 significantly reflects changes in what is counted, not only changes in who is homeless. The rising average length of stay suggests people are having a harder time exiting homelessness, even as headline numbers fall.

Policy & Legislative Timeline

Portland City Council · Maine Legislature
This section documents policy actions as factual events. Status labels (Passed, Active, Proposed, etc.) are descriptive, not evaluative. Where policies are contested, competing viewpoints are noted.
Jan 2025
Maine Legislature
Hotel Booking Fee to Fund Homeless Shelters (LD/HP 968)
A bill introduced in the 132nd Maine Legislature (First Special Session, 2025) would impose a fee on hotel, short-term rental, and RV camping reservations to generate revenue for operating homeless shelters. Sponsored by Rep. Gattine of Westbrook and Sen. Tipping of Penobscot, among others.
Proposed
Nov 2025
City of Portland, ME
Public Camping Ordinance Enforcement Resumed
Mayor Keith Wilson resumed enforcement of Portland's public camping ordinance on November 1, 2025. Officers can issue citations (up to $100 fine or 7 days in jail) to individuals camping in public spaces when "reasonable alternate shelter" is available. Officers have real-time shelter availability data and are directed not to cite individuals if no shelter space exists. The stated goal is to redirect people toward shelter and services. Advocates raised concerns about penalties, court system barriers, and shelter conditions that make congregate settings difficult for some individuals.
Active enforcement
Apr–May 2025
Maine Legislature
Bill to Protect Belongings During Encampment Sweeps
A bill advanced in the Maine Legislature requiring state and local governments to provide notice and hold hearings before removing a homeless person's belongings from public property. Exceptions include items reasonably believed abandoned or posing health/safety risks. The bill was described as protecting civil rights and ensuring procedural protections equivalent to those given to housed residents' property.
Advanced in committee (2025)
Feb 2025
City of Portland, ME
Camping Ordinance Enforcement Paused
Mayor Wilson's administration paused enforcement of the camping ordinance in February 2025, between the end of the prior mayor's enforcement period and a new enforcement strategy. The pause ended in November 2025.
Enforcement paused
Fall 2024
MaineHousing + Gov. Mills
$2M+ Warming Center Grants
Governor Janet Mills and the Maine Legislature approved over $2 million in grants distributed by MaineHousing for the operation of warming centers during the 2024–2025 winter season, following the expiration of pandemic-era emergency shelter funding.
Passed + funded
Sept 2024
City of Portland, ME
Initial Enforcement of May 2024 Camping Ordinance
Enforcement began in September 2024. Under Mayor Ted Wheeler, enforcement was limited and reserved for cases where a person simultaneously violated "manner" restrictions and refused an offer of shelter. No sweeping enforcement occurred.
Enforcement began
May 8, 2024
Portland City Council
Revised Public Camping Ordinance — Unanimously Passed
The Portland City Council unanimously passed Mayor Ted Wheeler's revised camping ordinance. It replaced a 2023 ordinance that was legally blocked. The new ordinance removed time-and-place restrictions, retaining only "manner" restrictions (e.g., no gas heaters, no blocking access, no obstruction of right-of-way) and prohibiting camping when "reasonable alternate shelter" is available. The council also required police to publish demographic data on enforcement actions, and directed regular policy review.
Passed unanimously
Nov 2023
Federal Court
2023 Camping Ordinance (TPM) Legally Blocked
Portland's prior "time, place, and manner" (TPM) camping ordinance was legally challenged by five homeless Portlanders and subsequently blocked by a court injunction. The City declined to appeal due to the time involved, prompting the 2024 rewrite.
Legally blocked
Jun 2024
U.S. Supreme Court
City of Grants Pass v. Johnson — National Ruling
The U.S. Supreme Court ruled that cities may penalize people for sleeping or camping on public property without violating the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on cruel and unusual punishment, even if no shelter is available. This ruling, while from Oregon, directly affects how Maine cities can legally regulate camping. Maine also has a state law limiting such penalties when no shelter space is available — any changes to that would require state legislative action.
Federal ruling (impacts Maine)
Mar 2023
City of Portland
New Homeless Services Center Opens (Riverton neighborhood)
The City opened its new Homeless Services Center in the Riverton neighborhood, replacing the Oxford Street Shelter. The facility provides 179 new beds, daytime services, lockers, outdoor space, and is served by a shuttle to downtown Portland. Meals are provided by Preble Street's Food Security Hub.
Opened
2022–ongoing
State + City + Nonprofits
Asylum Seeker Transitional Housing Program — Saco
The State of Maine, City of Portland, and Catholic Charities Maine launched a transitional housing program for asylum-seeking families in Saco. As of late 2024, the program had served 192 households (737 individuals). 81% of those who left transitioned to permanent housing. The program is expected to close in September 2025. Nine of 25 remaining families have signed leases.
Winding down (2025)

Currently debated / under discussion

Federal funding uncertainty: Governor Mills warned in November 2025 that over 1,200 at-risk Mainers could lose housing under proposed federal changes. Federal heating assistance programs (LIHEAP) were paused, with MaineHousing providing stopgap funds. The future of HUD and federal homelessness grants under ongoing federal budget negotiations remains a major uncertainty for Portland's service providers. Preble Street issued a statement in 2025 noting 1,200 Mainers at risk of re-entering homelessness due to anticipated federal changes.
Site-based Housing First expansion: Maine's 132nd Legislature included funding to support the creation of more Site-based Housing First programs across the state. Avesta Housing has secured an option on the former Oxford Street shelter site for a planned 30–50 unit mixed-income development with permanent supportive housing.
Maine state camping/encampment law: Maine has a state statute limiting cities from penalizing people for sleeping outside when no shelter is available. Some state lawmakers have signaled interest in revisiting this law following the Grants Pass Supreme Court decision. No legislation has passed as of 2025.

Organizations & Services

Portland, ME · Active providers
Preble Street
Shelter, Food, Housing, Advocacy
Maine's largest homeless services nonprofit (est. 1975). Operates shelters, food pantry, social work services, teen shelter, women's shelter, Housing First programs, and statewide advocacy. ~300 staff. Four shelters including three in Portland, one in Bangor. Partners with Avesta Housing on permanent supportive housing.

City of Portland — Health & Human Services
Emergency Shelter, General Assistance
Operates the Homeless Services Center (Riverton), Family Shelter, and coordinates city-level response. Administers General Assistance program. Maintains real-time shelter availability data. Coordinates with police, outreach workers, and service providers.
portlandmaine.gov →
MaineHousing
State Agency — Funding & Data
Independent Maine state agency administering federal and state housing funds. Conducts the annual Point-in-Time count. Distributes grants for shelter operations, affordable housing development, and housing assistance programs. Primary source of statewide homelessness data.
mainehousing.org →
Avesta Housing
Affordable Housing Development
Largest nonprofit affordable housing provider in northern New England. Owns and maintains Housing First buildings (Logan Place, Florence House, Huston Commons) in partnership with Preble Street. Operates 1,000+ affordable homes at 25+ Portland properties. Planning new development at former Oxford Street site.
avestahousing.org →
Catholic Charities Maine
Transitional Housing, Asylum Seeker Services
Operated the Saco transitional housing program for asylum-seeking families in partnership with the City of Portland and the State of Maine. 81% permanent housing success rate. Also provides immigration and refugee services, food pantries, and social services statewide.
ccmaine.org →
Opportunity Alliance
Workforce, Mental Health, Services
Community action agency providing wraparound services at the Homeless Services Center including workforce re-entry programming, mental health support, and financial stability services.

Amistad
Behavioral Health, Peer Support
Provides behavioral health peer support services at the Homeless Services Center and across Portland. Works with individuals experiencing mental illness and substance use disorders who are experiencing homelessness.

Community Health Center
Federally Qualified Health Center offering primary care, mental health, and substance use treatment to unhoused individuals in Portland. Works with shelter-based case managers to connect clients to MaineCare, medications, and ongoing care.

Civil Rights Advocacy
Publishes Know Your Rights guides for people experiencing homelessness in Portland. Has challenged camping ordinances on constitutional grounds. Advocates for civil rights protections during encampment sweeps, including property storage rights.

The federally designated body that coordinates homelessness services across Maine's Continuum of Care regions. Administers HUD funds, coordinates the annual Point-in-Time count with MaineHousing, and manages the Homeless Management Information System (HMIS).

Glossary of Terms

Point-in-Time (PIT) Count
A federally mandated annual count of homeless individuals on a single January night. Required by HUD for communities receiving federal housing funds. Conducted in Maine by MaineHousing and the Maine Continuum of Care. Captures sheltered and unsheltered homelessness but misses many informal living situations.
Unsheltered
Individuals sleeping in places not meant for human habitation — streets, parks, encampments, vehicles, abandoned buildings. Distinct from those in shelters or transitional housing. Counted separately in the PIT.
Chronically Homeless
HUD definition: an individual with a disabling condition who has been homeless continuously for 12+ months, or on at least 4 occasions in the last 3 years totaling 12+ months. This population has complex needs and is prioritized for Housing First programs.
Housing First
An evidence-based approach that prioritizes placing people directly into permanent housing without preconditions (such as sobriety or treatment compliance), with supportive services provided afterward. Preble Street's Logan Place, Florence House, and Huston Commons operate on this model.
Site-based Housing First
A variant of Housing First in which an entire building is dedicated to formerly homeless individuals, with on-site 24/7 professional social work staff. This model is used by Preble Street and Avesta Housing at multiple Portland properties.
HMIS (Homeless Management Information System)
A federally required database tracking individuals accessing homelessness services, including shelter admissions, housing placements, and service utilization. Used to generate data for HUD reports and to coordinate care across providers. Managed locally by the Maine Continuum of Care.
General Assistance (GA)
A Maine program administered by municipalities that provides short-term financial assistance to individuals and families in immediate need, including for emergency housing. Overseen at the state level but funded and operated at the municipal level.
Transitional Housing
Temporary housing (typically up to 24 months) with supportive services designed to help individuals and families move to permanent housing. Distinct from emergency shelters (short-term) and permanent supportive housing (long-term).
Continuum of Care (CoC)
A regional body designated by HUD to coordinate a community's approach to ending homelessness. Responsible for organizing funding applications, data collection, and planning across a defined geographic area. Maine has a single statewide CoC.
Reasonable Alternate Shelter
Under Portland's camping ordinance, a person can be cited for camping in public only if they have access to "reasonable alternate shelter." This may include congregate shelters, transitional housing, or hotel placements assessed as suitable for the individual. Officers have real-time data on bed availability.
MaineCare
Maine's Medicaid program, providing health coverage to low-income individuals. Many unhoused individuals are eligible but may have lapsed coverage; shelter-based case managers frequently help clients reinstate MaineCare as a first step toward accessing health and behavioral health services.
Rapid Rehousing
A short-to-medium-term intervention that provides rental assistance and services to help homeless individuals and families quickly move into private market housing. Differs from Housing First in that support is time-limited. Preble Street is expanding rapid rehousing services to Lewiston.
