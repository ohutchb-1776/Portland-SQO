Understanding Point-in-Time (PIT) counts: Federally mandated snapshot of homelessness on a single January night. Captures sheltered and unsheltered individuals — but misses people doubled-up, couch-surfing, or in informal motel arrangements. MaineHousing explicitly cautions that the apparent decline "does not necessarily mean fewer people are actually facing homelessness" — pandemic-era motel counts inflated 2022–2023 figures, and their expiration deflates 2024–2025 figures. Read trends with care.

Maine housing gap study
"We are running out of runway to meet the demand. We are running out of space, our staff is stretched too thin, and we cannot expect our taxpayers to continue to shoulder the financial burden involved in providing these services where half of the unhoused come from outside of the city."
— Mayor Mark Dion, State of the City Address, Jan. 6, 2025, Portland, Maine
Why is the number falling but the situation still dire? The 38% increase in average shelter stay length (from 8.4 days in 2023 to 11.6 days in 2024) means people are having a harder time leaving shelter for permanent housing — the exits are getting harder, even as the raw headline count drops. Maine's housing supply is short by an estimated 38,500 units today, with 84,000 additional units needed by 2030.
Racial disparities: Black, African American, and African individuals are significantly overrepresented in Maine's unhoused population relative to their general population share. American Indian/Alaska Native and Multi-Racial residents are similarly overrepresented. The 2025 count recorded a notable decrease in this group (1,141 → 757), partially attributed to successful permanent housing transitions through the Saco transitional program (81% success rate, 192 households).

Key figures — MaineHousing 2025

  • 2025 PIT total: 2,413 — down 282 (10%) from 2024
  • Families with children: 14.9% of all households; <0.4% unsheltered
  • Veteran count increased ~10% from 2022–2025 statewide
  • Saco transitional program (Catholic Charities Maine): 81% permanent housing success rate
  • 775 affordable housing units built in Maine in 2024; 727 more projected for 2025
  • Avg. shelter stay rose 38% in 2024 — flagged as "concerning shift"
  • Maine housing demand outpaces supply by 38,500 units currently
  • Portland issued 4,500 building permits in 2024 — highest in years (Mayor Dion, Jan. 2025)

What the data does not capture

"Hidden homelessness" — people doubled-up in overcrowded housing, couch-surfing, or in informal motel arrangements — is not counted in PIT totals. MaineHousing explicitly warns that the headline decline from 4,411 (2022) to 2,413 (2025) largely reflects what is counted changing (end of motel programs), not only fewer homeless people. The rising average shelter stay — from 8.4 to 11.6 days — suggests exits from homelessness are becoming harder, not easier.
Preble Street on 2025 federal risk: In 2025, Preble Street stated that 1,200 Mainers are at risk of re-entering homelessness due to anticipated changes in federal housing and social services funding, including proposed HUD Continuum of Care grant cuts and pauses to federal heating assistance (LIHEAP). Governor Mills issued a warning about this risk in November 2025.

Policy & Local Actions

Portland City Hall · Mayor Dion · Maine DHHS · 2022–2025
All policy events below are documented facts from Maine sources only. Status tags are descriptive. Where contested, both sides are noted factually.
May 2025
City of Portland
v. Maine DHHS
Portland Sues Maine DHHS Over Shelter Funding Cut
Mayor Dion announced that the City of Portland filed suit against the Maine Department of Health and Human Services over General Assistance Proposed Rule No. 26. Under the rule, the state would reimburse cities $48/night per shelter guest — a reimbursement of 70% — down from Portland's previous rate of $84/night. Dion says this cuts $4.4 million from Portland's shelter budget. "This isn't trimming your budget or tightening your belt a little bit — this is cutting us in half," Dion said. "We're going to court." 62% of HSC guests come from communities outside Portland; Dion argues the shelter provides a statewide need and should receive corresponding state support. DHHS spokesperson Lindsay Hammes said the rule "reasserts its longstanding practice for shelter reimbursement."
Active litigation
Jan 2025
Mayor Dion
State of the City
Dion: Funding Crisis Warning, Calls for State Coordination Office
In his Jan. 6, 2025 State of the City address, Mayor Dion declared Portland is "running out of runway." He called on the Portland state delegation to press Augusta for more funding. He reported Portland sheltered 2,000 people in 2024 with 600 beds filled nightly across three shelters. Dion also called for a dedicated state office to coordinate homelessness data and resources across municipalities — noting there is a statewide council of mayors but no mechanism to share shelter resources between cities. He also asked the City Council to reassess the "Green New Deal" inclusionary zoning ordinance, arguing the $178,000/unit fee may be suppressing housing construction.
Ongoing priority
Nov 2025
Mayor Dion
News Center ME
Dion on Homelessness, Addiction, and Conditional Housing
In a November 2025 interview with News Center Maine, Mayor Dion described his view that drug addiction is "the overriding cause" of homelessness in Portland. He expressed support for "conditional housing" — requiring individuals to take steps toward rehabilitation as a condition of placement. He noted the HSC is near capacity every night with all but 10–15 of 258 beds occupied. He reiterated his frustration that surrounding communities contribute little to Portland's shelter costs. He also expressed concern about panhandling near busy intersections, calling it "a fatality waiting to happen." Portland's large encampments are gone, but unsheltered individuals remain present throughout the city daily.
Ongoing policy view

Gov. Mills
Maine
Warning: 1,200+ Mainers at Risk Under Federal Changes
Governor Janet Mills warned that proposed federal budget changes could cause over 1,200 at-risk Mainers to lose housing. Federal LIHEAP heating assistance was paused; MaineHousing used state funds as a stopgap. Preble Street issued a parallel statement citing 1,200 Mainers at risk of re-entering homelessness due to expected changes in federal CoC grants, which fund permanent supportive housing for roughly 218,000 people nationally.

2024
City of Portland
Park Rangers + Police
809 Tents Removed; Camping Ordinance Enforcement (Maine Law)
The City of Portland removed 809 tents from public land in 2024, carried out by park rangers and police officers providing advance notice. Portland's camping ordinance — passed unanimously by the Portland, Maine City Council — prohibits public camping when "reasonable alternate shelter" is available. Maine state law separately restricts municipalities from penalizing outdoor sleeping when shelter is unavailable. City policy directs officers not to cite individuals if no beds are open. Mayor Dion reported in late 2025 that no large encampments remain in Portland, though unsheltered individuals are still present throughout the city. Data has shown that tent removals often relocate people rather than moving them into shelter.
Active enforcement
2023
ACLU of Maine
Know Your Rights — Portland Encampment Protections
The ACLU of Maine publishes "Know Your Rights" materials documenting Portland-specific protections for unhoused individuals during encampment sweeps: 24-hour advance written notice required; personal property must be stored for at least one week; property cannot be destroyed unless abandoned or a health/safety hazard; if all Portland emergency shelters are full, the city cannot remove campsites except for immediate hazards or obstruction. These rights apply in Portland, Maine specifically and may differ in other Maine municipalities.
Active rights framework

The City opened the $24–25M Homeless Services Center at 654 Riverside Street in Portland's Riverton neighborhood, replacing the former Oxford Street Shelter in Bayside. Designed by Winton Scott Architects, built by Cianbro. 258 beds. LEED Gold certified, solar powered, fossil-fuel free. Individual bed clusters with charging stations, private shower stalls, commercial kitchen, health clinic, community garden. 2022–2025
State + City + Catholic Charities ME
Asylum Seeker Transitional Housing — Saco, Maine
A joint program of the State of Maine, City of Portland, and Catholic Charities Maine serving asylum-seeking families at a Saco hotel. Served 192 households (737 individuals). 81% of those who completed the program transitioned to permanent housing. The program wound down in fall 2025. Widely cited as a successful model of coordinated government-nonprofit transitional housing. Nine of 25 remaining families signed leases as the program closed.
Concluded — strong outcomes
Jul 2023
Gov. Mills
Maine Legislature
Site-Based Housing First Funding Signed Into Maine Budget
Governor Janet Mills signed a supplemental budget package including funding to support the creation of 12–15 new Site-based Housing First programs across Maine, modeled on Preble Street's Logan Place, Florence House, and Huston Commons. Avesta Housing subsequently secured a purchase option on the former Oxford Street Shelter site for a planned 30–50 unit mixed-income permanent supportive housing development.
Signed into law
Jun 2024
U.S. Supreme Court
City of Grants Pass v. Johnson — National Ruling
The U.S. Supreme Court ruled that cities may impose civil or criminal penalties for sleeping or camping in public without violating the Eighth Amendment — even if no shelter is available. This ruling is from a case originating in Oregon and does not directly change Maine law. Maine has a separate state statute limiting municipalities from penalizing outdoor sleeping when no shelter is available. Any change to that Maine law would require action by the Maine Legislature. Some Maine lawmakers have signaled interest in revisiting the state law; no legislation has passed as of 2025.
Federal ruling — Maine law still limits enforcement

Policy approaches being debated nationally — context for Portland

The following approaches are factual policy debates occurring nationally and in comparable cities. Included for informational context only. Each has documented proponents and critics — both sides noted where relevant.
Housing First
Permanent housing without preconditions
Place people directly into permanent housing regardless of sobriety or treatment compliance; provide services after. Maine has funded expansion via the 2023 budget. Preble Street has operated this model in Portland for 20+ years. Pro: Houston reduced homelessness 60% over a decade. Con: Some argue accountability expectations improve long-term outcomes for individuals with addiction.
Conditional Housing
Housing linked to rehabilitation steps
Mayor Dion's stated position. Requires individuals to take steps toward rehabilitation as a condition of housing placement. Pro: Ensures accountability; some programs (e.g. Step Denver) show results with accountability-linked services. Con: Housing First evidence base shows better outcomes for unconditional placement; preconditions create barriers to the most vulnerable.
Tourism / Lodging Tax
Fund shelters via hotel/rental fees
Maine's LD 1476 proposed this mechanism. Pro: Ties tourism economy to social infrastructure; diversifies funding beyond property taxes (Mayor Dion: "fair, innovative, and fiscally responsible"). Con: Maine tourism industry opposed; Maine House voted it down 92–51 in June 2025. Used in Colorado for affordable housing.
Zoning Reform
Allow more housing density
Mayor Dion asked the Portland City Council to reassess the Green New Deal inclusionary zoning ordinance, arguing the $178,000/unit fee suppresses construction. Pro: More housing supply lowers prices over time. Con: Opponents argue affordability requirements ensure new units are accessible; removing them could produce market-rate-only development.
Regional Coordination
Share shelter data and costs across municipalities
Mayor Dion has specifically called for a dedicated state office to coordinate homelessness data and resources between cities. 62% of Portland HSC guests come from outside Portland; surrounding municipalities provide little funding. Pro: Matches cost-sharing to regional need. Con: Requires state legislative action and political consensus across municipalities.
Non-Police Crisis Response
Mental health responders for homeless-related calls
Modeled on Eugene, OR's CAHOOTS program (1989) and Denver's STAR program (2020). Trained civilian crisis workers + medics respond instead of police for non-violent calls. Pro: Reduces criminalization; Brookings finds better outcomes than police-only or co-responder models. Con: Requires separate staffing and dispatch infrastructure. Not yet established in Portland, ME.
Real-Time Data Systems
Move beyond annual PIT counts
Community Solutions' "Built for Zero" model tracks homelessness by name in real time, allowing targeted intervention. Mayor Dion has called for a state coordinating office with live data. Pro: Chattanooga effectively ended veteran homelessness using this approach. Con: Requires sustained investment in data infrastructure and privacy protections.
Maine State Camping Law
Revisiting the shelter-availability requirement
Maine's state law currently limits municipalities from penalizing outdoor camping when no shelter is available. Post-Grants Pass Supreme Court ruling, some Maine lawmakers have signaled interest in revisiting this. Pro: Would give cities more enforcement flexibility. Con: Critics argue removing this protection would criminalize homelessness without providing housing — and data shows sweeps move people, not out of homelessness.

PIT: 3,325 (2025) · Harris, Fort Bend, Montgomery counties
Houston's "The Way Home" coalition (est. 2011) aligned city, county, nonprofit, and philanthropic funding around Housing First with real-time coordinated entry data. No sobriety or treatment requirements for housing placement. Mayor John Whitmire launched a $70M "Initiative to End Street Homelessness" in 2025. In 2024, there were more veteran move-ins to housing than new veteran homelessness in Houston.
▲ 60% reduction in homelessness over a decade. BUT: unsheltered population surged 16% in 2025 as federal COVID relief expired — demonstrating Housing First requires sustained funding to hold gains. Lesson: works long-term, but federal funding loss reverses progress quickly.
Denver, CO
Mixed approach
PIT: 6,539 (2024) · metro: 9,977
Denver invested $100M+ in non-congregate hotel shelters and micro-communities, reducing outdoor encampments 23%. Launched the STAR program (mental health + paramedic non-police response) in 2020. Connected 2,000+ outdoor sleepers to housing since 2023. Metro Denver Homeless Initiative has used real-time coordinated entry data since 1994.
▲ Total homelessness rose 12% in 2024, but unsheltered family homelessness dropped 82.5% and unsheltered individuals in Denver proper fell 10.5%. Lesson: targeted investment and real-time data can reduce specific groups even as aggregate numbers rise.
Boston, MA
New England comparable
PIT: ~7,700 (2024) · cold-weather, high-cost, comparable economy
Boston operates Pine Street Inn (~700 beds — one of New England's largest) alongside a large nonprofit network. Massachusetts has a legal right-to-shelter for families, which placed enormous strain on capacity with asylum seeker arrivals. Massachusetts relies heavily on state-funded emergency assistance alongside city shelter.
▲ Homelessness increased substantially 2023–2024 driven by asylum seekers. Lesson: a legal right-to-shelter guarantees access but requires proportional funding to avoid system overload — a dynamic directly relevant to Portland, ME's regional burden.
Chattanooga, TN
Built for Zero model
Veteran homelessness: functionally ended by 2020
Chattanooga Regional Homeless Coalition used Community Solutions' "Built for Zero" model — real-time by-name tracking of every unhoused individual rather than annual snapshots. Targeted specific sub-populations (primarily veterans) with intensive coordinated services. In 2024, more veterans exited homelessness in Chattanooga than became homeless.
▼ Functional zero veteran homelessness achieved locally. Lesson: sub-population focus with real-time data can achieve measurable milestones — even without solving all homelessness. Directly applicable to Maine's veteran count increase.
New York City, NY
Enforcement-heavy
Shelter system: 140,000+ · PIT up 53% in 2023–2024
NYC operates the largest municipal shelter system in the U.S. and has a legal right-to-shelter. ~2,300 encampment sweeps were conducted Jan–Sept 2024. Of nearly 3,600 individuals affected, only 114 were placed in shelters and none in permanent housing. The Coalition for the Homeless calculated $3.6M spent on sweeps in 2024 could have housed 175 individuals already approved for supportive housing.
▲ Homelessness rose sharply; sweep-to-housing conversion rate extremely low. Lesson: enforcement without proportional housing supply moves people within the system — it does not resolve homelessness.
Austin, TX
Housing-focused
One of few major metros with documented declines (2023)
Austin invested heavily in permanent supportive housing and rapid rehousing with strong regional coordination between city, county, and surrounding municipalities. Brookings cited Austin alongside Indianapolis as a national bright spot. Strong job market and newer housing stock provide structural advantages.
▼ Homelessness declining — warm-weather city with strong economy and regional coordination. Lesson: regional cost-sharing and sustained housing investment produce results. Maine's climate and economy differ significantly.
Brookings Institution (2024): Punitive policing of homelessness — arrests or fines for camping, sleeping, eating in public — makes it harder to solve by adding criminal records that impede housing access and employment. Non-police crisis response (Denver STAR; Eugene CAHOOTS) outperforms both police-only and co-responder models for behavioral health calls. Regional, data-driven coordination with real-time tracking produces better outcomes than siloed city-by-city approaches.
National Alliance to End Homelessness (2025): Federal rollback of CoC funding, HUD programs, and housing vouchers threatens 218,000 people in Permanent Supportive Housing nationally. Veteran programs demonstrate that when adequately funded with clear targets, homelessness can be reduced to functional zero for specific populations. Maine's statewide single CoC is an asset in this regard.
Community Solutions "Built for Zero": The primary barrier to progress in most cities is the lack of real-time data. Annual PIT snapshots prevent timely intervention. Cities that track homelessness by name in real time can identify and respond before situations worsen. This approach is not yet in use in Portland, ME, though Mayor Dion has called for a state coordinating office with live data capabilities.
A cautionary note: Houston's experience illustrates that even a highly successful 10-year program can unravel quickly when federal funding changes. Sustainable homelessness reduction requires consistent, long-term public investment — a lesson directly relevant to Maine as CoC grants and LIHEAP face federal uncertainty in 2025.

Glossary of Terms

Point-in-Time (PIT) Count
Federally mandated annual count of homeless individuals on a single January night. Conducted in Maine by MaineHousing and the Maine Continuum of Care. Captures sheltered and unsheltered homelessness but misses doubled-up and informal situations. A snapshot — not a comprehensive measure.
Unsheltered
Individuals sleeping in places not meant for human habitation — streets, parks, encampments, vehicles, abandoned buildings. Counted separately in the PIT from those in shelters or transitional housing.
Chronically Homeless
HUD definition: an individual with a disabling condition who has been homeless continuously for 12+ months, or on 4+ occasions in the last 3 years totaling 12+ months. Prioritized for Housing First and permanent supportive housing programs.
Housing First
Evidence-based approach placing people directly into permanent housing with no preconditions (sobriety, treatment compliance, income). Services are provided after housing. Preble Street has used this model in Portland for 20+ years. Maine's 2023 budget funded 12–15 new Housing First programs statewide.
Site-Based Housing First
A Housing First variant where an entire building is dedicated to formerly homeless individuals with 24/7 on-site professional social work staff. Preble Street + Avesta Housing use this model at Logan Place (2005), Florence House (2010), and Huston Commons (2017) in Portland.
General Assistance (GA)
Maine program administered by municipalities providing short-term financial assistance for emergency housing and basic needs. The state reimburses a share of municipal costs. Proposed Rule No. 26 would cut Portland's reimbursement rate from $84 to $48/night — a $4.4M cut Portland is now challenging in court.
HMIS
Homeless Management Information System. Federally required database tracking individuals accessing homelessness services — admissions, housing placements, service utilization. Managed by the Maine Continuum of Care. Used to generate HUD reports and coordinate care across providers.
Continuum of Care (CoC)
A HUD-designated body coordinating community homelessness response — funding, data, planning. Maine has a single statewide CoC (unusual nationally; most large states have multiple regional CoCs). Administers federal CoC grants, coordinates PIT counts, manages HMIS.
Reasonable Alternate Shelter
Under Portland, Maine's camping ordinance, an individual can only be cited for public camping if they have access to "reasonable alternate shelter." Portland police officers have real-time bed availability data to make this determination. If all shelters are full, enforcement is not authorized under current city policy.
Built for Zero / By-Name List
A methodology from Community Solutions tracking every homeless individual by name in real time rather than annual snapshots. Allows targeted intervention and measurable outcomes. Chattanooga used this to functionally end veteran homelessness. Not yet adopted in Portland, ME — Mayor Dion has called for a state-level coordinating office with live data capabilities.
MaineCare
Maine's Medicaid program. Many unhoused individuals are eligible but have lapsed coverage. Shelter-based case managers at the HSC and partner organizations frequently help clients reinstate MaineCare as a first step toward accessing health and behavioral health services.
Rapid Rehousing
Short-to-medium-term rental assistance and services helping homeless individuals move quickly into private market housing. Time-limited — differs from permanent Housing First. Preble Street is expanding rapid rehousing services in Maine. LD 1287 would create a similar Housing Stability Support Program through MaineHousing.
